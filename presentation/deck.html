AI in Business:
Beyond Efficiency
Does AI create real business value?
Dordevic · Kuralov · Chen
The central question
Working faster does not
automatically create more value.
If you bill by the hour, fewer hours mean lower revenue. If you bill at a fixed price, fewer hours mean higher margins.
The mechanism linking AI, speed, quality, and pricing model matters more than speed itself.
Thesis in one slide

AI productivity is not automatically business productivity.

The project shows that saved hours become value only when the contract, quality process, and pricing model allow the agency to capture them.

Core question: does AI create real business value, or only make work faster?

Productivity effect
Hours fall.
Average task time moves from 13.3h to 10.8h at 60-80% AI.
Value capture
Pricing decides.
Hourly work gives efficiency away; fixed and value-based work can convert it into margin.
Operating rule
40-60% is safest.
Best outcome score before rework risk accelerates above 60% AI usage.

Methodology
A five-step pipeline from raw tasks to business decision
01
Load
Import the task-level CSV and verify shape, columns, and key business fields.
02
Clean
Normalize categories, parse dates, and keep the latest version of duplicate tasks.
03
Engineer
Create margin, capped margin, rework ratio, loss flag, and five AI bands.
04
Compare
Analyze speed, quality, rework, loss rate, pricing model, seniority, and task type.
05
Control
Run numpy OLS to check the AI association after covariates.
Design principle: descriptive patterns first, then robustness checks and a controlled association model. The result is evidence for a pilot, not causal proof.

Mix decomposition

Recalculating every AI band with the 0–20% pricing mix changes the pattern only slightly. The pricing mix gap stays around 0.3-0.5 percentage points, so the aggregate loss-rate drop is not just a composition effect.

Hourly contracts remain the riskiest model. Fixed and value-based contracts show much lower loss rates once AI usage reaches the middle bands.

These are exploratory group averages, not causal estimates. The pattern suggests senior work may be higher-quality but structurally more costly or more complex.

Why this matters

Seniority is a confounder. Seniors show the strongest outcome scores, but not the strongest margins. That is exactly why the controlled model is needed before making business recommendations.

A strong presentation should not hide uncomfortable results: quality, effort, and margin are different outcomes.

Operational recommendation
The sweet spot is 40–60% AI usage:
maximum outcome score, strong margin,
rework still under control.
Above 60%: additional QA should be mandatory (rework >30% almost triples). Below 40%: speed is left on the table.
Concrete decision
One-quarter pilot: convert top hourly accounts to fixed prices
Pilot scope
  • High-AI, high-revenue hourly accounts
  • Keep AI usage in the 40–60% band during the pilot
  • Benchmark: 35.7% fixed-contract margin as comparison
Stop conditions
  • Average margin below 10% after one full quarter
  • Outcome score below the 40–60% benchmark
  • More than 30% of tasks with rework ratio > 0.30

AI use in the project
AI was useful when it challenged the first interpretation
Discovery
Speed was not the real story.
AI helped move the analysis from “hours go down” to the stronger mechanism: pricing decides whether saved time becomes value.
Correction
The first loss-rate reading was too simple.
The mix decomposition made the conclusion more rigorous: loss-rate improvement is not explained only by a different pricing mix.
Prompt strategy
Ask for mechanisms, not summaries.
The strongest prompts were: “what explains this pattern?”, “what could confound it?”, and “what decision follows from it?”

This is why the final project includes descriptive analysis, robustness checks, and a controlled association model: AI was most valuable as a critical thinking partner, not as a replacement for statistical judgment.

Limitations & future work
What this analysis cannot answer

Current limitations

  • Observational data — no causal proof, AI usage was not randomly assigned
  • Unknown AI tool — tool used, prompt count, and prompt quality were not recorded
  • Unattributed rework — we do not know whether it was caused by AI or other factors
  • Client approval — first-delivery approval rate is not in the dataset
  • Fragile 80–100% band — only 48 tasks, treat as directional evidence

Data to collect next

  • AI-attributed rework reasons
  • Prompt-quality scores per task
  • Client first-approval rate
  • Before/after data from a controlled pricing pilot
  • Tool-level logs (which AI, how often)
Key conclusions
AI productivity is not automatically
business productivity.
1
AI reduces hours systematically. The 60-80% AI band uses 18.8% fewer hours than the low-AI baseline.
2
The pricing model determines whether saved hours become margin or lost revenue.
3
Quality is highest at 40–60% AI. Above 60%, rework almost triples (14.5% → 47.9%).
4
The right action is not to maximize AI usage, it is to convert pricing and monitor quality.
